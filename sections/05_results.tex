\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Primary Sweep}
\label{sec:primary-sweep}     

SimPO is the only method that significantly beats the best ICL baseline at $n = 100$ on both datasets --- by 3.6 points on GoodReads ($p = 0.002$, paired Wilcoxon) and 5.9 points on Netflix ($p = 0.011$). Figure~\ref{fig:primary_curves} and Table~\ref{tab:primary_acc} show the full picture, and the same two-regime shape appears on both datasets. At $n \leq 10$ the trained methods sit at or below the frozen base model and the ICL baselines lead. The crossover lies between $n = 10$ and $n = 50$. From $n = 50$ onwards the trained methods overtake, with SimPO clear of the pack and IPO close behind.

\subsection{Beyond the Cold-Start Cap}

Pushing $n$ from $100$ to $150$ on GoodReads pulls the trained methods toward ceiling and leaves the ICL plateau where it was. Mean accuracy at $n = 150$ is $0.983$ for SimPO, $0.958$ for IPO, $0.932$ for DPO and $0.920$ for KTO, against $0.806$ for \texttt{icl\_flat}. The pairwise ordering established at $n = 100$ in §5.1 is preserved, but the gap between SimPO and the rest of the trained methods narrows as everything approaches the upper bound of the evaluation.

\label{tab:extended_acc}

Two notes qualify the comparison with §5.1. The extended sweep was run on a different draw of 22 GoodReads users from the same eligibility pool, with ten users in common with the primary sweep, so the $n = 150$ column is best read as a separate measurement on the same population rather than as a continuation of any individual user's curve. And \texttt{icl\_chat} is not reported here: at $n = 150$ the prompt-side context already exceeds what the chat template can carry under the $8{,}192$-token cap, and the method's accuracy collapses to a level that reflects truncation rather than model behaviour. The \texttt{icl\_flat} variant remains within its working range at this size.

\subsection{Per-User View}

The mean curves in §5.1 hide whether the gain is spread across users or concentrated in a few. Figures~\ref{fig:per_user_gr} and \ref{fig:per_user_nf} show the full per-user grids: each panel is one user's learning curve under each method, on the same axes as Figure~\ref{fig:primary_curves}. Two facts emerge from the grid that are not visible in the means. No user is harmed by training at $n = 100$: on both datasets, the best trained method beats the frozen base model for every one of the twenty-two users. And no user is left stuck near chance: the worst best-method accuracy is $0.933$ on GoodReads and $0.600$ on Netflix.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$\beta$-Sensitivity}{beta-Sensitivity}}
\label{sec:beta-sensitivity}

The $\beta$-sensitivity sweep checks whether the \S\ref{sec:primary-sweep}
ordering among the three reference-anchored losses is an artefact of
$\beta = 0.1$. Across the three values tested on GoodReads at $n = 50$,
no method moves more than four percentage points and the four-method
cluster stays well above the cold-start baselines, but the ordering
inside the cluster is not stable. SimPO leads at $\beta = 0.05$ ($.826$)
and $\beta = 0.10$ ($.860$), and KTO overtakes it at $\beta = 0.5$
($.824$ vs $.811$). DPO and IPO sit within one to two points of each
other at all three values. KTO is the most $\beta$-sensitive of the
four: it is fourth at $\beta = 0.1$, second at $\beta = 0.05$, and
first at $\beta = 0.5$. The headline finding from
\S\ref{sec:primary-sweep} --- that the reference-anchored losses are
clearly above the ICL baselines at $n = 50$ --- is therefore stable in
$\beta$. The internal ordering of the four trained losses is not, and
the \S\ref{sec:primary-sweep} ranking among them should be read as the
result at the literature-default $\beta$ rather than as a robust ranking
across temperatures. A multi-seed $\beta$ sweep would be required before
reading more into the differences.

\subsection{Prompt-Template Ablation}
\label{sec:prompt-ablation}

The prompt-template ablation re-runs the comparison under two alternative
phrasings to check whether \S\ref{sec:primary-sweep} reflects a property
of the methods or of the \texttt{simple} template. The broad two-regime
picture --- ICL and SimPO at the top, the other trained losses behind ---
is preserved on both alternatives. SimPO is the best trained method under
all three templates; \texttt{icl\_chat} is the best method overall under
\texttt{simple} ($.883$) and \texttt{system} ($.888$); and
\texttt{icl\_chat} beats \texttt{icl\_flat} on every template, which is
the cleanest evidence in the thesis that the chat template itself is
doing work beyond surface formatting. Under \texttt{detailed} the top
two swap: SimPO rises to $.871$ and edges past \texttt{icl\_chat}
($.853$), the only template phrasing on which a trained method beats
\texttt{icl\_chat} at $n = 50$. The one template-level effect worth
flagging is that the \texttt{system} role depresses the four trained
losses by roughly four to six points relative to \texttt{simple} while
leaving the ICL baselines roughly untouched, which is consistent with
the role-priming text competing with the LoRA signal for control of the
model's behaviour. The ordering at the top therefore depends on the
phrasing, but the qualitative picture from \S\ref{sec:primary-sweep} ---
\texttt{icl\_chat} and SimPO clustered ahead of the rest --- survives
every template.

\subsection{Classical Recommender Comparison}
\label{sec:results:classical}

The three classical baselines (Figure~\ref{fig:recsys_goodreads},
Table~\ref{tab:classical_acc}) check whether the \S5.1 ordering reflects
something the LLM family adds or just the level of any working
recommender at this scale. The ranking among the LLM methods is unchanged
by their inclusion, but the cold-start picture is narrowed in two specific
ways. At $n = 3$ the four trained losses sit at chance ($0.50$--$0.53$)
while POP --- which ignores the user's pairs entirely --- already reaches
$0.60$, and IKNN climbs from $0.60$ at $n = 3$ to $0.66$ at $n = 10$. The
ICL lead over IKNN at $n = 3$--$10$ is two to four points, not the
twelve-point lead it holds over the trained losses. The classical curves
then plateau where the LLM curves keep climbing: IKNN flattens around
$0.69$ from $n = 50$ onward and BPR around $0.77$, while ICL\_CHAT and the
four trained losses are clear of the classical pack by $n = 50$ and beyond
it by twenty-five points at $n = 100$. The additional climb is the part of
the signal that title semantics carry and that co-occurrence and matrix
factorisation cannot represent.

\subsection{Free-Form Generation}
\label{sec:results:judge}

The methods in this thesis are evaluated by scoring fixed pairwise completions, but a deployed recommender writes its suggestions as natural-language sentences. This sub-study (\cref{sec:exp:freeform}) asks whether the methods still rank the same way when the model must write a recommendation instead of score one. Figure~\ref{fig:flip} visualises the two metrics reported in Table~\ref{tab:judge_results}: the rate at which a Claude Sonnet judge prefers each method's sentence head-to-head, and the rate at which the title named in the sentence matches the book the user actually liked in the held-out set.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/updated/fig_flip.pdf}
\caption{The same three conditions evaluated under two formats. Left: held-out pairwise accuracy (fixed-completion scoring). Right: judge win-rate on free-form one-sentence recommendations. The ranking inverts: SimPO leads on scoring but \texttt{icl\_chat} leads on generation.}
\label{fig:flip}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[!ht]
\centering
\caption{Free-form generation sub-study on GoodReads, 10 users $\times$ 10 held-out pairs $= 100$ generations per condition. Judge win rate is across the three pairwise matchups; alignment is the rate at which the parsed choice equals the held-out liked title, with a one-sided binomial $p$ against chance.}
\label{tab:judge_results}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Judge win rate} & \textbf{Alignment rate} & $p$ vs chance \\
\midrule
\texttt{icl\_chat} ($n = 10$) & \textbf{.655} & \textbf{.790} & $2.2 \times 10^{-9}$ \\
simpo ($n = 100$)             & .460          & .590          & $.044$ \\
base                          & .385          & .550          & $.184$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The ranking flips. \texttt{icl\_chat}, which puts ten of the user's preferences directly into the prompt, writes the better recommendation on both measures and is the only condition whose alignment is clearly above chance. SimPO, trained on a hundred per-user preferences, writes recommendations that are statistically indistinguishable from the untrained base, and the two name the same book on $94$ of $100$ cells. The training changes the log-probability score the model assigns to a fixed completion but almost never changes the book the model would actually recommend, because SimPO learns to score a pre-written format it never has to produce. \texttt{icl\_chat} places the user's preferences in the prompt at generation time, where the model has to attend to them. The practical implication is that the offline accuracy used in §5.1 cannot be relied on alone: a method that wins on the benchmark may produce no improvement in the format the user actually sees.
