\section{Business Analysis and Decision Framework}
\label{sec:business}

\subsection{Market Tendencies}
\label{sec:business:tendencies}

The conversation about large language models has shifted over the last three years from "which model is the best" to "which model best fits this user." Frontier systems from OpenAI, Anthropic and Google sit within a few percentage points of each other on the public arenas \citep{chiang2024arena}, and the decisions companies make about which to deploy are increasingly driven by price, latency and integrations rather than raw capability. As performance commoditises at the base-model layer, the place where a product can still differentiate moves into how well the system fits the user in front of it. The industry's first answer has been context: Claude Projects, ChatGPT Memory and Gemini's long-context window \citep{reid2024gemini} are all expressions of the same bet, that a powerful enough frozen model can absorb a user's preferences from text alone. The bet holds up for ad-hoc tasks but strains when preferences accumulate over months, which is the regime where training a small per-user adapter starts to look attractive. The two paradigms are the strategies the market is actively trying out, and the question this thesis answers is when each is the right call.

\subsection{Business Applications}
\label{sec:business:framework}

This question concerns companies that already sit on the kind of discrete user signal classical recommender systems were built to consume: ratings, watch histories, purchase sequences, thumbs-ups and skips. The work of \citet{bao2023tallrec} and \citet{friedman2023recllm} shows this signal can be fed into an LLM either by training on it or by placing it in the prompt, and the survey by \citet{fan2024recllmsurvey} confirms this is the direction the field is moving. The question is no longer whether to do it, but how.

The answer is sharper than a single curve can show because the deployment format matters as much as the training size. The crossover in \cref{fig:primary_curves} tells a clean story when the product scores fixed options: at $n \leq 10$ ICL leads on both accuracy and cost; between $n = 10$ and $n = 50$ the trained methods catch the ICL plateau; and at $n = 100$ SimPO leads \texttt{icl\_chat} by 3.6 points on GoodReads and 5.9 points on Netflix. A music app ranking the next track, a streaming service ordering thumbnails, or a search engine re-ranking ten retrieved candidates is operating in exactly that format, and the §5.1 ranking is the operational ranking: stay in ICL until the average user crosses the band, then switch to a trained adapter.

The free-form sub-study (\cref{tab:judge_results}) complicates this picture. When the same models are asked to write a one-sentence recommendation rather than score a pair, the ranking inverts. \texttt{icl\_chat} at $n = 10$ wins the head-to-head judge comparison 65.5 percent of the time and names the user's held-out liked book 79 percent of the time ($p = 2.2 \times 10^{-9}$). SimPO at $n = 100$ drops to 46 percent on the judge and 59 percent on alignment, statistically indistinguishable from the untrained base; the two name the same book on $94$ of $100$ cells. The fixed-completion gap between SimPO and base, which is forty points on GoodReads, does not transfer to what the model says when asked to recommend. A LoRA adapter of rank 16 sharpens the log-probability margin in the format it was trained on but leaves the generation distribution essentially unchanged. The implication for the AI community is that pairwise accuracy on the training format is a partial proxy for personalisation in deployment, not a complete one, and benchmarks that report only the former may be over-stating the value of the methods they compare.

For products with a ranked-list surface, the §5.1 ordering is the right guide and trained adapters are worth deploying once $n$ crosses fifty. For products whose surface is a sentence written by the model, which describes most of what the LLM era has actually shipped, ICL is the dominant strategy across every $n$ tested and the burden of proof for switching to a trained method should sit on the team proposing the switch. The infrastructure does not change between the two paths: the data collected is the same, and the serving stack only diverges at the last step, with trained methods loading a small LoRA adapter on top of the same frozen base that ICL queries directly. \citet{sheng2024slora} showed that thousands of LoRA adapters can be served concurrently on shared infrastructure, which removes the operational objection that per-user training is impractical at scale. The choice to invest in data collection now does not commit the company to either path.

\subsection{Alignment Economics}
\label{sec:business:economics}

The economic case for each path turns on three variables: how many preferences a user generates during onboarding, how often they query the system afterwards, and what the model is asked to produce when serving them. Mapping the findings against the cost trajectories in the Stanford AI Index 2025 \citep{maslej2025aiindex} turns the abstract trade-off into a decision a product team can make, and the format axis changes the arithmetic more than the headline curves alone suggest.

Start with the case where the format favours training. The cost of running an inference at GPT-3.5 quality dropped from \$20.00 per million tokens in November 2022 to \$0.07 per million tokens by October 2024, a 280-fold reduction in roughly eighteen months \citep{maslej2025aiindex}. An ICL inference at $n = 100$ carries roughly four thousand extra prompt tokens compared to a trained-model inference; at a realistic mid-market rate of \$0.20 per million input tokens that adds about eight hundredths of a cent per query. A LoRA training run for one user on a rented A100 costs ten to twenty cents, which puts the break-even at roughly two hundred and fifty queries per user without prompt caching and around two thousand five hundred queries once prefix caching is in play.\footnote{ Anthropic's prompt-caching documentation reports a roughly tenfold cost reduction on cached prefix tokens: \url{https://docs.claude.com/en/docs/build-with-claude/prompt-caching}.} For a scoring product engaged in the hundreds of queries per user, these numbers favour training.

The generative case is different. The break-even above implicitly prices the value of a training run as the SimPO--\texttt{icl\_chat} accuracy gap at $n = 100$, which is real for fixed-completion scoring but not for generation. The free-form sub-study found that SimPO loses head-to-head against \texttt{icl\_chat} at $n = 10$ by 20 points on judge preference and 20 points on alignment, while naming the same book as the untrained base on 94 of 100 cells. The marginal value of the training run, measured in what the user actually receives, is between negligible and negative. Spending ten to twenty cents per user to obtain a model that writes the same sentence as the base and a worse sentence than the ICL prompt is not a break-even at any query volume; it is a loss at every query volume.

Two trajectory effects make the picture less stable than \cref{tab:business_regimes} suggests, and both lean further toward ICL. Inference costs are falling 9 to 900 times per year depending on the task \citep{maslej2025aiindex}, while hardware price-performance drops a steadier 30 percent per year, so ICL economics are improving faster than training economics and the break-even rises even for the scoring case. And smaller models are catching larger ones on standard benchmarks: the Llama-3-8B-Instruct used here is already capable enough to extract signal from book titles at $n = 10$ and to write better recommendations from that signal than a hundred-pair-trained adapter does. Two model generations from now, that capability will sit lower on the parameter curve and favour ICL further.

The recommendation is sequencing for scoring products and patience for generative ones. A ranking product is best served by starting with ICL, collecting paired preference data, and switching to a trained adapter for users past a clear engagement threshold. A generative product should start with ICL, collect the same data, and treat the decision to train as one that requires evidence the gain transfers to the actual output format, not as one that follows automatically from a pairwise benchmark. The open question for the AI community sits here: the methods studied sharpen the log-probability margin on the training format and leave the generation distribution largely untouched, and closing that gap, whether through larger LoRA ranks, training objectives that act on the generation distribution directly, or evaluation protocols that score what the model writes rather than what it ranks, is the next step for work that wants its claims to carry over from a paired-completion benchmark to a product.
