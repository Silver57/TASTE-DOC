\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Why This Matters}
\label{sec:intro:why}

Up to now, the way we interact with technology has mostly been constrained by the technology itself: first we had command-line terminals, then graphical user interfaces, and now we are entering a new phase where our interactions with machines are beginning to resemble our interactions with other humans. At the core of this transformation are LLMs pre-trained on enormous datasets and remarkably good at mimicking human communication using mathematics and vast computational power. However, the priorities that we had in the past remain largely unchanged. One of our central priorities over the last decade has been how do we know what to show someone based on what he likes, these are called recommender systems. Most companies do not train their own models because that is prohibitively expensive, but they still need to adjust them to their clients' needs. In the race to differentiate through personalization, they must achieve this alignment in a quick and cost-conscious manner.



\subsection{Where This Sits in the Current LLM Landscape}
\label{sec:intro:landscape}

As I said in the last section, LLMs take a great amount of effort to develop from scratch. There are a select few that are at the forefront of what's possible with LLMs, namely OpenAI (ChatGPT), Anthropic (Claude), and Google (Gemini). These models have become the gold standard but also really similar in performance \citep{chiang2024arena}. With the base capability becoming generalized, companies will look into shifting their attention to how the LLM fits the user in front of it through personalization methods, and the working assumption across the industry is that almost every model deployed in production will be personalized in some form within the next few years.

In this thesis I will be comparing two broad families of personalizing LLMs. One adjusts the model itself, training it on user preferences via a preference-optimisation loss (DPO, IPO, SimPO, KTO), typically applied through a lightweight per-user adapter so the cost of doing this per user stays low. The other leaves the model frozen and conditions it at inference time, placing the user's preferences directly in the prompt and letting the model attend to them on the fly, the family known as in-context learning (ICL). \Cref{sec:bg} covers how each of these works in detail. Even though they have a similar goal, over the last few years there has been a clear separation in their usage, DPOs have become known for aligning tone and general-helpfulness, while ICL has been the go-to for tasks and examples passed to the models. A head-to-head comparison of the modern preference losses against in-context learning in the single-user cold-start regime --- where a product can plausibly collect only a handful of preferences before deciding how to personalise --- has not, to my knowledge, been carried out. That is the gap this thesis fills.



\subsection{Research Questions and Contribution}
\label{sec:intro:rq}

\begin{quote}
\itshape
 In the cold-start regime, we only have a limited number of explicit preferences; how does the choice of preference-alignment approach affect an LLM's ability to predict that user's preferences on held-out items?
\end{quote}

My aim is to clearly identify what the best alignment method is in identifying user taste in a cold-start environment, be it in terms of performance, simplicity and cost.

\subsection{Approach}
\label{sec:intro:approach}


My approach is to focus solely on a recommender-style benchmark where the different methods (DPO, IPO, SimPO, KTO, and two in-context learning variants) are given a number of user preferences and asked to pick some more. This way I can compare all the methods consistently, focusing on the actual taste of each user. I work with two famous datasets, GoodReads (books) and Netflix (movies), that are widely used in this type of comparison and contain information indicating users' taste. For each user I either fine-tune the Llama-3-8B-Instruct model I am using (DPOs) or paste the preferences straight into the prompt (for ICL), sweeping the training size from 3 to 150 preference pairs so I can see how sample-efficient each method is. I run every configuration on 22 users per dataset across multiple random seeds and also introduce prompt ablations, so the differences I see between methods reflect the methods themselves and not just a lucky prompt or a lucky initialization.
